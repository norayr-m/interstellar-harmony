What it is

A browser visualization that runs a small reasoning system as three concentric phases on a ranked grid. The board is laid out as an onion shell, with rank 6 as the outermost ring and rank 0 at the centre. Tokens enter on the outer ring, spiral inward through the ranks during processing, stabilise at the centre, then spiral back outward through the ranks and emerge as decoded output.

The whole loop runs as a single continuous animation. You press SOLVE and watch encode → process → decode play out as one motion. The reasoning engine in the middle is a small knowledge-graph + pattern-solver pair — not a real language model — chosen so the dynamics are simple enough to follow visually.

The aesthetic is borrowed from Hans Zimmer’s score for Christopher Nolan’s Interstellar (2014): the organ in the dust, time as a physical dimension, information moving through layers we do not directly inhabit. The aesthetic is decoration; the structural content is the rank-as-time encode/process/decode geometry.

Why it matters — bio digital twins

A bio digital twin is a computational model of a specific biological system — a section of cortex, a hepatic lobule, a heart chamber — wired tightly enough that it can predict the system’s response to a stimulus. Many biological systems can be naturally described by a rank coordinate: position from the periportal vein to the central vein in a hepatic lobule (16 zonation levels), distance from the centre of a cortical column (6 cortical layers), depth from the epicardium to the endocardium in a ventricular wall. The framework’s question is whether the rank coordinate can be treated as a time-like axis along which information flows inward (encoding) and outward (decoding).

The orbital transformer makes that abstract question concrete on a small example. If you can build a useful encode/process/decode loop on a 6-rank visual demo, the shape of the architecture transfers to a ranked tissue substrate where the same encode/process/decode geometry has biological meaning (gradient information from the boundary, processing in the interior, response signal back out to the boundary).

The visualization is deliberately a small example so the geometry is legible. Building real biological versions of this shape is the bio digital twin work; the visualization is the playground for the geometry that those tissues share.

The framing for the trio (generator + cell simulator + scanner) is the trio white paper. This visualization is not part of the trio — it is a standalone illustration of the rank-as-time geometry, with companions in drt-pipeline (same encode/decode shape on BWV 847) and drt-orbital-solver (3D orbital framing of the same skeleton).

How it works — three phases on a ranked grid

The substrate is a ranked grid with seven concentric ranks — rank 6 outermost down to rank 0 at the centre. Each rank holds a fixed number of cells; the outer ranks hold many cells, the inner ranks hold fewer, and the centre is a single integration node.

Three phases play out in order:

Phase 1 — Encode

Input tokens appear on rank 6. They are the raw signal entering the system from the boundary. Each token carries a small payload that the engine will read at the next rank inward. Visually, tokens light up around the outer ring.

Phase 2 — Processing

Each token spirals inward, rank by rank. At every rank step the token interacts with the local pattern store (a small knowledge graph held at that rank) and the local rules (small per-rank transformations). This is the “thinking” phase. Visually, you see the spiral path drawn in real time as each token moves from rank to rank, leaving a trail.

When all tokens reach the centre, the central rank computes the aggregate output of the processing. This is the moment at which the system has done its work; the inward spiral phase is complete.

Phase 3 — Decode

The aggregate at the centre is now read out: tokens spiral outward through the ranks, carrying the decoded output back to the boundary. The outward spiral mirrors the inward one — same ranks, same rules, reversed direction. By the end of the phase, the decoded output has reached rank 6 and the loop is complete.

The structural framing: the rank coordinate is playing the role of time in a typical transformer or recurrent network architecture. A token “going inward” is the same shape as a token “going forward in time through layers” in a deep network. A token “going outward” is the backward / decode path. The encoder and decoder share the same substrate; the only difference is the direction of motion along the rank axis.

Reading the visualization

When you open the live demo:

  • The board appears as a clean onion shell with seven concentric rings.
  • Press SOLVE. Tokens appear on the outer ring.
  • The encode phase plays: tokens light up as they enter rank 6.
  • The processing phase plays: tokens spiral inward, leaving visible trails. The display shows which rank each token is currently at.
  • The centre integrates the tokens; a brief pause indicates the aggregate is being computed.
  • The decode phase plays: tokens spiral outward, the trails reverse direction.
  • The decoded output appears on the outer ring at the end of the cycle.

What to look for:

  • Rank-as-time symmetry. The inward and outward spirals use the same substrate. The number of rank steps inward equals the number outward.
  • Aggregate at the centre. The single integration node is the analogue of an aggregate in the framework. Whatever it computes from the tokens is what the decoder reads out.
  • Token trails as a record of processing. The visible trails are the per-token paths; they are the most direct way to see what the engine is doing on a given input.
  • Pattern store influence. Different inputs follow different inward paths because the per-rank pattern stores route them differently. The visualization makes this routing visible without requiring you to read the engine’s source.

Companion work

This visualization sits with two repositories that explore the same encode/process/decode shape on different substrates:

  • drt-pipeline — same forward/backward shape applied to Bach’s BWV 847 fugue, with composer and decomposer running side by side on the same audio timeline.
  • drt-orbital-solver — 3D orbital-mechanics framing of the same encode → solve → decode skeleton, with observers on orbital paths and reconstruction at convergence points.

All three demonstrate the same conceptual question — can the encode/process/decode loop be made symmetric and decomposable on a small enough example to fit in a browser? — on three different substrates: ranked grid, audio timeline, 3D orbital scene.

What is not in the paper

The honest list of what this paper deliberately does not claim:

  • A working language model. The reasoning engine in the centre is a knowledge-graph + pattern-solver pair, not a trained transformer or any other modern language model. The “orbital transformer” name refers to the geometric shape of the architecture (encode → process → decode on a ranked substrate), not to a competitive language modelling system.
  • A norm-growth claim. Earlier prose around this family of demos asserted that the aggregate of many partial views had bigger norm than the original signal — that ∥R(f)∥ > ∥f∥. That inequality has been retracted in the v0.1 draft. The current claim is conditional and weaker: under four hypotheses and a bounded computational budget, the aggregate exposes structural features that are not accessible from the original signal alone within the same budget. The orbital transformer is a visual playground for the encode/process/decode shape, not a proof of the conditional claim.
  • A general theorem about rank-as-time. The visualization treats the rank coordinate as time-like because the substrate is small and clean. Real biological tissues with rank coordinates have additional structure (asymmetries between inward and outward paths, noise, irregular topology) that the visualization does not address.
  • A music-theoretical contribution. The Hans Zimmer aesthetic is decoration; the soundtrack reference does not extend any musicological claim. The repository name and styling are tribute, not analysis.
